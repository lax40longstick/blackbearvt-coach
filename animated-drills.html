Why animation matters

Step-by-step animation beats static diagrams

Most hockey-coaching tools render a single static diagram with arrows. That works for a coach explaining the drill to other coaches. It doesn't work for a 10U player trying to understand what they're supposed to do at the third cone.

Static diagram

Player sees five arrows, three labels, and one puck. They guess at the order of events. Half the team runs the drill wrong on rep one, the coach explains again, and ice time disappears.

Animated sequence

The drill plays back as four labeled steps. Players see the read happen in time. The first cone hits, the second arrives, the pass goes, the shot finishes. Step labels are coaching cues — "shoulders sell the fake before hands move."

Coach freedom

You can play the drill at the whiteboard before practice, on a phone at the bench, or projected in the locker room. The animation is the same in all three places. No re-drawing, no whiteboard erasers, no cold markers.

Sample drills

Three animated drills from the library

2v1 Rush Read: Pass or Shoot (q05) · 10U+ · Intermediate

Two attackers vs one defender. The animation walks the rush in four steps: drop pass slows the play, wide forward attacks D outside-in, cross-ice pass triggers as D rotates, late attacker arrives in shooting lane. Cues teach the read before the result.

Coaching points: Attack with speed · Make the read before hashmarks · Support away from pressure · Finish with rebound pressure

3v3 Below the Tops Score Game (q76) · 10U+ · Intermediate

Cross-ice 3v3 below the tops of the circles. Sequence shows always-two-options support, moving the puck through pressure, net drive creates time, defenders pressure stick-on-puck. Short shifts force decisions.

Coaching points: Short shifts · Support triangles · Quick decisions · Play through possession changes

Cross-Crease Track Drill (q96) · Goalie · 12U+ · Advanced

Goalie-specific. Eyes lock on pass before it leaves the stick, T-push slide stays square, set pads on second post before the shot, hold seal until puck is fully covered. Coaches get clear teaching cues; goalies get a repeatable tracking pattern.

Coaching points: Set feet before release · Track puck with eyes first · Recover square · Control or steer rebounds
