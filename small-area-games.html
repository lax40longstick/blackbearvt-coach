SAG · 8U through HS

Small-Area Games That Teach Hockey IQ Faster Than Any Drill

Drills teach skills. Games teach decisions. Small-area games — SAG for short — compress the decision-making rate so players make 4-6× more reads per minute than they would in a full-ice scrimmage. That's why every elite program uses them.

Why SAG works

Smaller surface = faster decisions = better players

Compressed decision rate

In 5v5 full-ice, the average player handles the puck for 30-45 seconds per game. In 3v3 cross-ice, that same player gets 90-120 seconds of touches in a 4-minute block. Decision reps explode.

Compete every shift

You can hide in 5v5. You cannot hide in 2v2. SAG forces every player to be on or off the puck — there's no third option of "watching the play."

Hockey-specific conditioning

SAG is interval training that doesn't feel like conditioning. 90-second shifts at full intensity, 90 seconds rest, repeat. The conditioning is a side effect of the game.

Short shifts force behavior change

Long shifts let players coast. SAG demands short, hard shifts — which is exactly the shift length your team should be playing in real games anyway.

Five SAG examples

Games every youth coach should know

All five are in the BenchBoss animated drill library with full step-by-step playback. Drill IDs included.

1. 3v3 Below the Tops Score Game (q76) · 10U+ · Easy setup

Setup: Cross-ice. Two nets at the goal lines. Game played below the tops of the circles only — pucks above the tops are dead.

Why it works: Forces players to attack with support and play through pressure. The "below the tops" rule eliminates long-range shots and east-west drift.

Coaching cues: Always two passing options · Move puck through pressure, not into it · Net drive creates time for support · Sticks first on defense

2. 3v3 Gates Game (q36) · 8U+ · Best for skill development

Setup: Cross-ice with two scoring "gates" (pairs of cones) instead of nets. Score by skating the puck through the gate — no shots allowed.

Why it works: Removes the shooting outlet, which forces puck protection, support, and decision-making. Creates the longest sustained possessions of any SAG.

Coaching cues: Score by skating, not shooting · Use hips to protect puck · Switch sides quickly when defenders collapse · Constant motion

3. Continuous 4v4 Change of Possession (q37) · 10U+ · Best for transition

Setup: Cross-ice. New pucks fed in by the coach every 30-45 seconds, regardless of game state. Old puck dies, new puck is live.

Why it works: Constant transitions force support shape. Players never get to "set up" — they have to be in position the moment the new puck arrives.

Coaching cues: Always know where the next puck is · Support before pressure · Quick decisions · Play through possession changes

4. King of the Hill 1v1 (q74) · 10U+ · Highest compete

Setup: Two players, one loose puck, one net. First goal wins. Loser becomes defender on the next rep. Line of waiting players, fast rotation.

Why it works: Pure compete. Every rep is a battle for body position and second effort. Players play for pride, not just for the coach.

Coaching cues: Body position before stick · Win the inside lane · Second effort wins · Skate to the puck, don't reach

5. Reverse Score Keepaway (q77) · 10U+ · Best for puck protection

Setup: Cross-ice 3v3 keepaway. Possession scored by counting full passes — every 5 consecutive passes = 1 point. Defenders score by intercepting and completing 3 passes themselves.

Why it works: Removes shooting entirely. Puck-protection and pass-receiving become the only path to points. Builds the patience habit that beats forecheck pressure.

Coaching cues: Hands away from body · Find support before panic pass · Use the boards as a teammate · Talk before pressure arrives

Scoring rules

How to make a SAG teach what you actually want

The game's scoring rule is the coach's biggest tool. Same 3v3 cross-ice setup, different scoring rules, completely different lessons.

Scoring rule What it teaches
Goal counts double if scored off a one-touch passTape-to-tape passing, off-puck movement
Goal only counts if scored within 5 seconds of zone entrySpeed of attack, no perimeter cycling
Goal only counts off a reboundNet drive, second-effort plays
Tap-in goals only (puck must be touched in the crease)Net-front presence, screens, tips
Defensive team scores by completing 3 passes after takeawayBackcheck habit, support after turnover

Coach mistakes

The 3 ways coaches ruin a SAG block

Long shifts

SAG only works with 60-90 second shifts. Long shifts ruin the intensity and turn the game into a slow scrimmage. Use a whistle, not vibes.

Coaching during the rep

Coach between shifts, not during them. The game is the teacher. If you're stopping every play to explain a read, you're killing the decision rate that makes SAG work.

No scoring rule

"Just play 3v3" without a scoring rule means players default to whatever bad habit they already have. The scoring rule is the lesson plan.
